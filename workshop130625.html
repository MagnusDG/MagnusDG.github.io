What Are We Talking About?

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

The broad science of making machines smart.

Machine Learning (ML)

A subset of AI where machines learn from data.

Large Language Models (LLMs)

The specific type of AI we're focusing on. They are trained on vast text data to understand and generate language.

Popular Models: GPT series (OpenAI), Claude series (Anthropic), Gemini (Google).

How an LLM *Really* Works

It's not magic. It's a sophisticated word prediction game.

1. It learns relationships: By reading billions of texts, the LLM learns that the word "sky" is often related to "blue," "clouds," and "sun," but rarely to "submarine."

2. It weighs context: When you give it a prompt, it pays "attention" to the key words. In the sentence "The capital of Taiwan is ___," it knows "capital" and "Taiwan" are the most important clues.

3. It predicts the next word: Based on the clues and the relationships it has learned, it calculates the most statistically probable word to come next. It's not "thinking," it's calculating.

How to Maximize an LLM's Power

Your goal is to reduce the AI's guesswork as much as possible.

Weak Prompt = High Guesswork

"Tell me about TSMC."

The AI has to guess: Should it talk about history? Financials? Technology? For what audience? The result is often generic.

Strong Prompt = Low Guesswork

"Explain TSMC's role in the global semiconductor supply chain to a first-year business student."

The AI has clear instructions on the topic, perspective, and audience. The result is focused and useful.

You are the director. The LLM is the actor. Give clear directions.

Core Prompting Techniques

1. Zero-shot vs. Few-shot Prompting

"Zero-shot" is a direct command. "Few-shot" provides examples to guide the AI, which is more powerful for specific formats.

Few-shot Example:
Q: What is the capital of France? A: Paris.
Q: What is the capital of Japan? A: Tokyo.
Q: What is the capital of South Korea? A:

2. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

For tasks requiring logic, ask the AI to "think step-by-step." This forces it to show its work and improves accuracy.

"...calculate the total cost. Let's think step-by-step to get the right answer."

A Simple, Powerful Framework: R.O.L.E.S.

Use this structure to build excellent prompts every time.

R - Role: Assign a specific persona. "Act as a senior marketing analyst..."

O - Objective: State the primary goal clearly. "...your objective is to analyze customer feedback..."

L - Logic: Provide step-by-step instructions. "...First, identify the top three themes. Second, find a representative quote for each. Third, suggest one improvement."

E - Examples: Give a small example of the desired output. "...for example: Theme: Delivery Speed, Quote: 'It arrived in one day!', Suggestion: 'Highlight fast shipping in ads.'"

S - Specifications: Define the format, tone, and constraints. "...present the output as a Markdown table. The tone should be professional. Do not exceed 300 words."

Applications: From Basic to Advanced

Level 1: The Information Assistant

Task: Summarize a long article

"Act as a research assistant. Read the following article and provide a 5-bullet-point summary of its key findings. [Paste article]"

Task: Explain a difficult concept

"Explain the concept of 'quantum entanglement' to a high school student who is interested in physics. Use an analogy to make it easier to understand."

Level 2: The Thought Partner

Task: Create a personalized study plan

"Act as an academic advisor. Here is the syllabus for my 'Introduction to Macroeconomics' class [paste syllabus]. I have 8 weeks until the final exam. Create a weekly study plan that covers all the topics, including revision sessions and practice quizzes."

Task: Critique an argument

"Act as a critical thinking expert. Read my argument below. Identify the main claim, then point out three potential logical fallacies or weaknesses in the reasoning. Suggest ways to strengthen the argument. [Paste your argument]"

AI for Lifelong Learning & Research

AI is your ultimate learning companion.

1. Become a Skill-Building Engine: Use AI to create learning plans for any skill you want to acquire.
"I want to learn basic Python for data analysis. I know nothing about coding. Create a 4-week learning plan with daily topics, key concepts, and a small project for each week."

2. Use Specialized Tools for Research: For literature reviews, tools like Elicit and Consensus are more powerful than general chatbots because they are connected to databases of scientific papers.

3. Stay Updated: Ask AI to act as your personal intelligence analyst.
"Summarize the top 5 most important developments in artificial intelligence from the past month. Explain why each is significant. Provide links to reliable sources."

Final, Critical Rules for Responsible Use

1. AI is a Starting Point, Not an End Point: Use it to generate ideas, drafts, and summaries. The final work, analysis, and critical thought must be yours.

2. Always Verify Critical Information: LLMs can "hallucinate" (make up facts). Never trust specific data, statistics, or claims without checking them against a primary source.

3. Protect Your Privacy: Do not input sensitive personal or confidential information into public AI tools.

4. Understand Your University's Policy: Every institution has its own rules on academic integrity and AI. Know them and follow them.
